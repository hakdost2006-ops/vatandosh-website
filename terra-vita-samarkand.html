Why this matters

Animal welfare in Uzbekistan is at a turning point.

Uzbekistan has growing public awareness and stronger legal protection for animals, but shelters and volunteer networks still carry much of the practical burden. Food, medicine, sterilization, adoption outreach, and public education remain urgent needs.

Key message

Sustainable progress requires moving from reactive sheltering toward humane, evidence-based animal population management that protects animal welfare, public health, and community trust.

Overview 2026

Animal protection, shelters, and legislation in Uzbekistan

Uzbekistan faces a significant challenge in managing stray and abandoned dogs and cats in urban and peri-urban areas. Awareness has grown, but the animal protection system remains underdeveloped and heavily dependent on volunteers and non-governmental organizations.

Current landscape

There is no official nationwide census of stray animals. Local groups and authorities report substantial free-roaming dog and cat populations in cities including Tashkent, Samarkand, Bukhara, Andijan, Namangan, and Fergana.

  • Low sterilization rates and pet abandonment drive overpopulation.
  • Unregulated breeding and limited public awareness add pressure.
  • Weak identification and registration systems make planning difficult.

Shelter system

Uzbekistan does not yet have a comprehensive national shelter network. Shelters are primarily operated by NGOs, volunteer groups, private individuals, and small charitable foundations, with limited municipal capacity in some locations.

  • Common needs include funding, food, medicine, staffing, transport, quarantine space, and rescue equipment.
  • Many shelters depend on social media fundraising and volunteer labor.

Veterinary and public health needs

Affordable sterilization and emergency veterinary care remain uneven, especially outside major cities. Humane animal population management also supports rabies prevention, bite prevention, zoonotic disease control, waste management, and community safety.

Legal and policy context

Animal cruelty became a criminal offense in Uzbekistan in 2021, and legal protections have improved. Implementation remains uneven because reporting mechanisms, investigation capacity, enforcement, pet registration, and municipal resources are still developing.

Strategic priorities

  • Expand affordable sterilization and vaccination.
  • Build shelter and veterinary capacity.
  • Improve data collection, registration, and transparency.
  • Strengthen enforcement of anti-cruelty laws.
  • Promote adoption, fostering, and responsible ownership.
  • Coordinate government, civil society, veterinarians, and international donors.

Urgent funding gaps

  • Food and medicine
  • Sterilization and vaccination
  • Transport and rescue support

International donors can have high impact through mobile sterilization clinics, humane population management, shelter capacity building, veterinary training, public awareness, adoption programs, and data systems.
